Undergradute Research

August 2020 - May 2021

Troy, NY

During my sophomore year, I worked with Dr. Nidhi Rastogi and Dr. Alex Gittens on a series of Natural Language Processing and Optimizaion projects that focued on malware threat analysis and truth detection.


Going into this reasearch opportunity, I did not have much experience with natural language processing, but I was familiar with malware characteristics, so we spent the first part of the research time reviewing papers on entity extraction and relationship prediction. Then we worked on developping our own entity and relationship extraction technique that was specifically for threat reports. This was done in Python, primarily using the Pytorch, Numpy, Matplotlib, and Scikit-Learn libraries. The knowledge graph part of this project was done with Neo4j, however I focued primarily on the entity and relationship extraction part. You can find the paper here.


The second major area focused on estimating trustworthiness. We examined an optimization algorithm from a paper by Google. The paper that we examined had ample training data to work with (i.e. multiple sources of the same fact to compare) while our dataset was much smaller. Therefore, our goal was to improve upon the algorithm so that it would work well on our smaller dataset. We developped the code base for the algorithm proposed in the paper (in Python). As we adjusted the algorithm, we found that when scalling it down, the opportunity for the model to converge was restricted and often times it would reach an unreasonable result. We were able to adjust the model so that it would converge for texts within our domain, however expanding outside of our domain (cybersecurity reports) was problematic and suffered in accuracy.

HackRPI

Nov 2019

Troy, NY

During my first semester at RPI, I competed in 2 hackathons (This is the second of which). For this hackathon I worked with 3 friends to develop an applicaiton and algorithm that could predict and find missing family members. This was designed to be used after a natural disaster to help account for people and reconnect seperated families. Our project was inspired by the 2012 film "The Impossible". Our goal was to make it as lightweight as possible run efficiently on weak wifi signal

I worked on the backend of the application (database configuration and querying) and the algorithm that was used to predict the links between missing family members. Due the time limit on development, the algortihm implementation was designed with restrictions like not accounting for specialized cultural naming convenctions. It would be improved to allow the orgnaization using the application to specify what region they are in so that preprocessing data would be less time consuming. Our submission can be found on the Devpost Page.
